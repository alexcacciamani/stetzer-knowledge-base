# How to Work with Architects as a Builder

This is one of the most frequently addressed topics across the entire Builder's Edge program. Erin returns to it repeatedly because the architect-builder relationship is foundational to project success, client experience, and long-term business growth. Here's a comprehensive synthesis:

---

## 1. Build Relationships Deliberately Over Years

Erin teaches that architects are **feeders** — primary referral sources for high-quality projects. Building these relationships is not a quick process; it requires sustained, intentional effort.

> "I identified five architects that I'm going to work with. And I identified five designers and I studied their work. I marched into their offices and said, I want to work with you. I don't know if I'm here yet, but if there's anything, any project you want me to bid on, I'm in."
> — *2024-03-20 — Builder's Edge Program Kickoff Call*

> "I make a list I'd still do this this year every year I have this list it will take me sometimes four years to get into the right architect four years I look back at my lists right so it's not a quick process but you have to know who that list is."
> — *2025-04-09 — Open Workshop: Feeders, Team Structure, Design-Build Strategy*

**Key strategies for getting in the door:**
- **Offer to price a project for free** just to demonstrate how you think, even if you don't win the job (*2025-04-09*)
- **Take their difficult or less desirable projects first** to prove your capabilities — get the principal to see your work directly, not just through a project manager (*2025-07-24 — Clarifying Your Ideal Client*)
- **Leverage your trade network** — ask flooring specialists, home automation pros, or other trades who work across many projects to introduce you by name (*2025-05-29 — Budget vs. Actual*)
- **Study their work deeply** before approaching them: understand their design language, their standards, and their client base (*2024-03-20, 2025-07-24*)

> "I stalked them on Instagram. I would ask a landscape architect to introduce me to an architect. I would work my trade base heavily. I was very much studying the architects and designers work and just trying to learn from these amazing people."
> — *2025-05-29 — Budget vs. Actual*

## 2. Position Yourself as a Collaborative Partner, Not an Adversary

Guest architect **Ken Newberry** (35+ years of experience) emphasized that the adversarial dynamic between architects and builders is counterproductive:

> "As far as I'm concerned, when we finish a project the client shouldn't know there were any problems at all. We did our job. We collaborated with the contractor and did a great job."
> — *2026-02-05 — Guest Speaker Ken Newberry*

Ken's core advice to builders:
- **Hire people better than you** — approach the architect relationship with humility, not ego
- **Present a unified front to clients** — align privately on strategy before client meetings so no one plays parties against each other
- **Learn 3D modeling** — architects will love you if you can identify structural-architectural conflicts before construction begins

> "If you learn 3D modeling you're going to figure out conflicts like a glue lam below a toilet or lighting or air conditioning ducts. Architects will love you if you know how to do 3D modeling."
> — *2026-02-05*

## 3. Share Your Systems and Process Early

One of Erin's most consistent teachings is that **your systems are what differentiate you** in an architect's eyes. When you show your organized approach — even if you don't win the job — architects remember.

> "We don't get the job. Well, now the architects and designers are saying, hey, let's go work with Aaron, Aaron Riddle, because you've shared this. And then it just sort of like gets bigger and bigger."
> — *2025-06-10 — Brand Identity and Social Media Consistency*

**What to share:**
- Your subcontractor pricing guide (*2025-04-17*)
- Your budget with price certainty columns (*2025-05-01*)
- Your weekly reporting format and four-week calendar (*2025-05-08, 2025-09-18*)
- Your modifications tracking process (*2025-05-15*)
- Your proposal booklet covering systems, scheduling, and communication (*2025-11-13*)

Presenting these systems signals to architects that their clients will be well cared for and that the project will be managed professionally.

## 4. Use the Subcontractor Pricing Guide as an Alignment Tool

Before reaching out to any subs for pricing, Erin's team creates a comprehensive finish schedule and uses it as **the basis for alignment meetings with the architect and designer**:

> "This worksheet here is a template for the conversations that we have with the architect and the designers prior to reaching out for pricing."
> — *2025-04-17 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide Walkthrough*

This document forces all parties to surface gaps, missing details, and unresolved decisions **before** they become field problems. Highlighted cells indicate unresolved items, giving the architect and designer a visual indicator of what's still needed.

> "When we receive an initial pricing set or a conceptual design we want to bring this worksheet to our meeting with our architect and our designer and we want to sit around a table and just say hey I'm trying to fill these pieces in could you help me."
> — *2025-04-17*

## 5. Lead the Design Decision Process — Don't Wait

Erin is emphatic that **builders must drive the timeline for design decisions**, not passively wait for architects and designers to deliver:

> "I actually don't expect anything. I'm going to take the lead and tell them what we need and when we need it and the way that we figure that part out is through the schedule."
> — *2025-12-04 — Open Workshop*

**Practical tools for this:**
- Use **two-week and four-week look-ahead schedules** to proactively communicate what decisions are needed and when
- Put outstanding design decisions on the **weekly client and designer report** and leave them there until resolved
- Explain the **downstream "why"** to designers — they don't naturally understand that a late tile selection delays the plumber, which delays the inspector, which pushes the move-in date

> "Builders are built to move projects forward crazy efficiently. Designers are built because they have extraordinary vision and creativity and they let it and things have to bubble... two different ball games."
> — *2025-12-04*

## 6. Handle Incomplete or Inadequate Drawings Professionally

This is a recurring pain point. Architects often deliver **60-70% complete plan sets** (*2025-04-04*), and some refuse to provide more detail until they know the project is in budget — creating a circular standoff (*2025-05-15*).

Erin's approach:

1. **Require a minimum "pricing set"** before providing any budget: floor plans, exterior elevations, cut sections, specifications, and allowances (*2025-10-16*)
2. **Document every engagement with the architect** with a clear reason — such as "the detail was not clear" or "dimensions on the plans don't match the elevations" — to protect yourself from construction administration billing disputes (*2024-03-20*)
3. **Hold your professional line**: If an architect won't provide adequate information, state clearly that you're not comfortable proceeding:

> "I'm not comfortable sending a modification to the client… or I'm not a fit. Like I will not operate like this. And I will say people respect that — that's a professional decision."
> — *2025-05-15*

Ken Newberry also addressed this from the architect's side:

> "Someone is going to have to make these decisions. Do you want the plumber to make the decision? Do you want the cabinet guy to make the decision? Someone has to make those decisions."
> — *2026-02-05*

## 7. Use Scorecards to Maintain Accountability

Erin scores every architect she works with on an ongoing basis and shares the scorecard framework **before** work begins to set mutual expectations:

> "Before we start a project, I show the scorecard. I said, these are the things that we're looking for in a partner."
> — *2025-08-21 — Scorecards*

Scorecards enable **resolution rather than termination** — when patterns of low scores emerge (e.g., three-day response delays), it triggers a direct, respectful conversation rather than letting frustration build.

> "Whenever I find myself riled up about something, I always separate the people from the problem."
> — *2025-08-21*

---

## 8. Proactive Deadline Management

Ken Newberry's advice on this was direct and practical:

> "Don't wait until the deadline to find out you're not going to meet the deadline. I tell my team that all the time. Ask five times between now and then: are you still on schedule, do you need anything from me?"
> — *2026-02-05*

**Documentation is the strongest accountability tool.** Recording what was promised, when, and in what meeting creates a clear record that drives follow-through without hostility.

## 9. The Long Game: Repeat Relationships as the Business Engine

As Erin's company matured, **90% of her business became repeat work with architects and designers** she had cultivated over years:

> "Most of our business, 90 percent, is repeat with architects and designers just because now we're gelled as teams."
> — *2025-05-29*

This is the endgame: a small number of deeply trusted architect-builder partnerships that produce pre-qualified, well-matched clients consistently, reducing the need for marketing, competitive bidding, or cold outreach.

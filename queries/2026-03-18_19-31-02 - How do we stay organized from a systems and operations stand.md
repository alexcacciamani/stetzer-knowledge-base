# Staying Organized: Systems and Operations in the Builder's Edge Framework

This is one of the most frequently addressed topics across the entire Builder's Edge program. Erin returns to it repeatedly because she believes systems are the foundational infrastructure that makes everything else — scaling, client trust, profitability, delegation — possible. Here's a comprehensive synthesis of how Erin teaches organizational systems across multiple dimensions.

---

## 1. The Core Philosophy: Simple, Repeatable, Practiced

Erin's overarching principle is that systems must be simple enough to actually use, and practiced consistently enough to become automatic. She is emphatic that complexity kills execution in construction.

> "We really simplify each of our processes because they work. And so if something, as you all know, if something works in this industry, you stay with it."
— **2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough**

> "You can have systems and if they're not practiced, it's not a system."
— **2026-01-22 — Scaling Your Business and Scaling Your Team**

She deliberately chooses Excel/Google Sheets and phone calls over project management software platforms like Buildertrend or Procore, arguing that most trades won't maintain digital systems and the data becomes unreliable:

> "We're 100% Excel and our, and it's a very simple — we really simplify each of our processes because they work."
— **2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough**

> "The language of construction is not email. We all know that. We can try to fight it all day long. But the deal is, it's a phone call in person, track you down."
— **2025-10-02 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide & Trade Tracker Walkthrough**

## 2. The Core System Architecture

Erin's organizational system breaks down into several interlocking playbooks, each with its own spreadsheet, cadence, and ownership. Here's how they fit together:

### A. Pre-Construction: The Subcontractor Pricing Guide

Before any pricing or bidding begins, Erin's team creates a comprehensive, room-by-room finish schedule that captures every project detail — foundation type, cladding, windows, MEP, interior finishes, and more. This is the **single source of truth** before trades are contacted.

> "This worksheet here is a template for the conversations that we have with the architect and the designers prior to reaching out for pricing."
— **2025-04-17 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide Walkthrough**

Key organizational rules:
- **Highlighted cells** indicate unresolved items, creating a visual map of how complete or incomplete the information is
- The **builder controls all data entry** — subcontractors never input directly into the document
- The Google Sheets version goes to architects and designers for collaborative input; a **locked Excel version** is distributed to trades

> "We don't let the subs input anything. We control the entire document so that we're in control of the interpretation."
— **2025-04-17 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide Walkthrough**

### B. Pricing Phase: The Trade Tracker

Once the pricing guide is complete, a color-coded **Trade Tracker** spreadsheet manages every subcontractor through a defined follow-up sequence:

- **Day 1**: Plans sent
- **Day 3**: Confirm receipt by phone
- **Day 5**: Check for questions
- **Day 10**: Escalate if nothing received
- **Day 11+**: Target quote receipt

> "These are the people involved in the tracking system. Because what happens is we want to avoid a situation where, hey, I thought you were doing this, or hey, I thought you were doing that."
— **2025-10-02 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide & Trade Tracker Walkthrough**

Each team member is assigned a **color code** so accountability is visible at a glance. The goal is to convert all cells to green. Target turnaround: **three to four weeks**.

> "The advanced move is to color code who's handling it. So if it's under reviewed and let's say Sarah handled that, we would turn this column pink."
— **2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough**

### C. Budgeting: The Conceptual Budget with Price Certainty

Erin credits one column — the **price certainty percentage** — with transforming her entire business. Each line item includes a number indicating how confident the team is in that figure: hard bid, historical data, allowance, or excluded (0%).

> "I just got really really mad one day and I'm like if nobody else is going to talk about this then I'm going to talk about it because no other builders are talking about this so I'm just going to explain how I got the numbers."
— **2025-05-01 — Creating a Conceptual Budget**

She later refined this into **three columns**:
1. Hard bid, ready to build
2. Bid with details yet to be developed
3. Historical data estimates

> "Column two is we have a bid. Details need to be developed… that means the money is not certain."
— **2026-01-22 — Scaling Your Business and Scaling Your Team**

The budget is deliberately called "conceptual" because it is a **living document** — never final until 100% of information is known.

> "Unless I have 100% of the information, this is controlled chaos. That's my personal approach. So there's never anything ever final in my world."
— **2025-10-16 — Budget Modifications Process**

### D. Construction Phase: Modifications Tracking

Once construction begins, the Subcontractor Pricing Guide is set aside. All changes are managed through a separate **modifications system**. Erin insists on the word "modifications" rather than "change orders" because of the psychological impact:

> "Change orders has a huge negative connotation. Like people, as soon as they hear changes, change order. And so maybe a year or two or three ago, we just said, hey, we're just going to call this modifications to a project."
— **2025-05-15 — Modifications Control the Process**

The system works as follows:
- Project managers document every change in the field immediately — even a text message or photo of a note on their palm is sufficient
- Changes are categorized by the **"Diamond of Why"**: (1) added/deleted, (2) design won't work in the field as drawn, (3) detail not yet developed at time of budgeting, (4) external forces like weather or market conditions
- **Every modification crosses the owner's desk** before going to the client
- A running **pending modifications** total is surfaced at every OAC meeting

> "One of the systems for us in modifications is that that particular modification has to go across my desk. So it can't just go straight to the client because I want to understand the situation really."
— **2025-05-15 — Modifications Control the Process**

> "It doesn't have to be fancy. It can be on my palm. You can shoot me a picture of whatever it is on your palm. Just get it in her hands."
— **2025-11-20 — Guest Expert Kelli Little on Construction Financial Systems**

### E. Construction Phase: Budget vs. Actual

During construction, a **Budget vs. Actual spreadsheet** tracks four components: original budget, modifications column, total budget (budget + modifications), and draw columns (previous draws, current draw, total draws, balance to finish, percent complete).

The critical discipline: **percent complete financially must align with percent complete on the construction site.**

> "Where projects get in trouble in big trouble is when the percent done on the construction site is 20 but the money we're asking from a client is at 70 percent. That's off."
— **2025-05-29 — Budget vs. Actual**

Only **real, approved numbers** go into the official spreadsheet. Hypothetical or projected changes go on a separate copy.

> "Unless it's a real thing and it's really approved, we don't load it into this spreadsheet. These are actual facts."
— **2025-05-29 — Budget vs. Actual**

### F. Weekly Reports and Four-Week Calendars

Every project manager sends a **weekly report** on the same day every week, without exception. This report includes:
- Work performed
- Work planned
- Material orders
- Approvals received
- Modifications
- Decisions needed from clients and designers
- Anticipated problems
- A **"red hot list"** of urgent items in red

> "My only goal in weekly reports is to not get a phone call or an email because I didn't explain something well."
— **2025-05-08 — Open Workshop: Lien Waivers, Modifications, Scaling**

Accompanying each report is a **four-week rolling calendar** — Erin's sweet spot for client-facing scheduling. Two weeks feels too short; six months is too speculative.

> "The four weeks is enough to give people closure on the details. Like they feel that things are getting done and they know they're getting done. But it doesn't extend to where we're not hitting our marks."
— **2025-05-08 — Open Workshop: Lien Waivers, Modifications, Scaling**

## 3. Organizational Infrastructure: File Management and Templates

Erin keeps everything housed in a **standardized template folder** so every new project starts with identical file names, templates, and structure. This means any team member can step into any project.

> "When we start a new project, it is the same file names, the same templates, everything used. So it's like, that's our go-to spot for documents. So people aren't pulling in a document from here and pulling in a document from here."
— **2026-01-22 — Scaling Your Business and Scaling Your Team**

Project videos and photos live in a **shared iCloud folder** organized by job. The business owner must create all shared folders to maintain ownership and access control.

> "Be sure that you as the business owner are the ones who start all those folders. Because if somebody else starts them, they own them and they're the only ones who can add people."
— **2025-05-08 — Open Workshop: Lien Waivers, Modifications, Scaling**

## 4. People and Role Clarity Within the System

Erin emphasizes that systems only work when people have **clear ownership** of specific functions. Her organizational model, refined over 25 years:

- **Project Managers**: Own the field — daily videos, action items, modifications documentation, weekly reports, client communication. They are expected to operate as if they own the company.
- **Money Manager (Accounts Payable)**: Receives invoices, enters them into QuickBooks, cuts checks, issues client draws. Stays in their lane.
- **Bridge Person**: Sits between PMs and money managers — collects modification information from the field, assembles formal modification documents, handles approvals. Also cross-trained in pricing and estimating.
- **Owner**: Watches cash flow, reviews every modification before it goes to clients, maintains the wide-angle lens on job sites, stays in the game strategically.

> "I don't want the project managers trying to put together a formal document. All I need is the information. So then what happens is the bridge person knows what's happening in the field and the bridge person can also explain this to the money person."
— **2025-09-04 — Cash Flow Projection and Business Financial Planning**

> "Cash flow keeps you in business. First hire is money person."
— **2026-01-22 — Scaling Your Business and Scaling Your Team**

She also insists on **cross-training** so there are no single points of failure:

> "Stay as lean as you can, as efficient as possible, have everyone cross-trained so that we can all cover for each other."
— **2025-11-06 — Open Workshop: Project Management, Scaling, Scheduling**

## 5. Daily Cadence and Calendar Discipline

For Erin, staying organized is not a weekly exercise — it's structured into every day. She teaches a **calendar-first approach**:

> "My entire calendar was every minute filled out to where I was going to be for that entire week. Repeat, repeat, repeat. I said, if anybody needs to talk to me, you will find me at that project at that time. I was not catering to everybody else's schedules."
— **2025-10-23 — Company Structure, PM Scorecards, and Financial Systems**

Project managers are required to follow an **"every house, every day"** rule — visiting every active project daily, even if only for five minutes. They enter from a **different point each visit** to force fresh observation, and record at least **three videos per day per project**.

> "Every house, every day, period. That is what happens. It's not negotiable."
— **2025-10-30 — Project Manager Playbook: Daily Cadence**

> "Enter from a different part of the home every single day. Because if you enter in the front door every single time as a project manager, you're not going to see things because it just becomes part of ho-hum."
— **2025-10-30 — Project Manager Playbook: Daily Cadence**

## 6. Invoice and Draw Management

A structured daily process ensures financial organization:
1. Invoices arrive in a **holding file**
2. The PM reviews and approves (or holds) each invoice
3. Approved invoices move to an **approved file**
4. The money manager assembles draws from the approved file
5. Multiple checkpoints are passed before any draw goes to a client: PM approval, money manager review, owner review, homeowner satisfaction, architect/designer sign-off

> "Not once a week, not every other week. It's every single day."
— **2025-10-23 — Company Structure, PM Scorecards, and Financial Systems**

Erin bills **twice per month** (first and fifteenth) for cost-plus clients to smooth cash flow, and staggers draws across the month so they don't all land simultaneously.

## 7. Scorecards as the Ongoing Feedback System

Erin uses **scorecards** for trades, architects, designers, and project managers as the living accountability system that ties everything together. They are filled out consistently (ideally weekly), reviewed for patterns, and shared at the **start of every new relationship** to set expectations.

> "Scorecards are literally the heartbeat of our business."
— **2025-08-21 — Scorecards: Building a Performance Evaluation System**

> "Before we start a project, I show the scorecard. I said, these are the things that we're looking for in a partner."
— **2025-08-21 — Scorecards**

> "If it's a two on that line item for four weeks, that requires a conversation with the team."
— **2025-08-21 — Scorecards**

## 8. The Continuous Improvement Loop

A recurring theme is that systems are never finished. Erin adds new systems constantly based on real project experiences:

> "We had two things come up in the last two weeks that are now part of our systems that we're implementing."
— **2024-03-20 — Builder's Edge Program Kickoff Call**

> "I make so many mistakes during the day. I just don't tell you. But it's just part of the deal… As long as you're making progress."
— **2025-06-19 — Open Workshop: Designer-Builder Integration**

She maintains a **shared cloud video library** where team members upload field discoveries, new techniques, and best practices as they are encountered — keeping institutional knowledge growing without waiting for formal training.

> "Every time we learn something in the field, it could be a waterproofing thing, it could be a new tile coming out, we share these things in this library so that people can access it and just keep learning."
— **2025-10-23 — Company Structure, PM Scorecards, and Financial Systems**

## Summary: The Organizational Stack

| System | Purpose | Cadence | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subcontractor Pricing Guide | Pre-pricing alignment | Per project, before bidding | Pricing team |
| Trade Tracker | Follow-up on bids | Daily during pricing phase | Pricing team (color-coded) |
| Conceptual Budget (with price certainty) | Client-facing budget presentation | Updated as information develops | Owner + pricing team |
| Modifications Tracker | Construction-phase changes | Daily documentation, owner review | PM → Bridge → Money Manager |
| Budget vs. Actual | Financial tracking during build | Updated with each draw | Money Manager |
| Weekly Report + 4-Week Calendar | Client communication | Weekly, non-negotiable | Project Manager |
| Scorecards | Performance accountability | Weekly | Owner |
| Cash Flow Projection | Business-level financial planning | Weekly minimum | Owner + Money Manager |
| Template Project Folder | Consistent file structure | Set up at project start | Standardized |
| Shared Video Library | Institutional knowledge | Ongoing | Whole team |

The throughline across **every session** is this: systems are what allow a builder to grow, maintain quality, delegate with confidence, and sleep at night. As Erin put it in the very first session:

> "I'm huge on systems and I'm huge on operations, consistency, showing up regardless of how you feel or think. Those are all systems."
— **2024-03-20 — Builder's Edge Program Kickoff Call**

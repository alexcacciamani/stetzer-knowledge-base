# Common Lessons Across Spec Home Building Sessions

Erin's Builder's Edge program includes several spec-focused roundtables and discussions. Synthesizing across all of them reveals a set of recurring themes that Erin and the spec builder participants return to repeatedly.

---

## 1. Sell Finished, Not Mid-Construction

This is perhaps the single most reinforced lesson. Across multiple sessions, builders who listed homes during construction consistently reported negative outcomes — accumulated days on market, buyer confusion, and depressed pricing.

> "You get one shot to hit the market. You want to hit the market at the right time of the year for your specific market, but finished, punch list done, staged, pre-inspected, ready to go." — **2025-11-11 — Spec Builders Roundtable: Financing, Margins, and Building Strategy**

The **2025-12-09 — Spec Home Selling Strategies** session reinforced this with a concrete cautionary tale: Tiffany's home accumulated 200+ days on market because it was listed during construction, signaling to buyers that something was wrong even though the issue was purely timing.

The group developed specific tactical responses: pull the listing off MLS, wait the required reset period, and relist when finished and staged. As one participant put it:

> "Change your photos. The description doesn't matter. Buyers will look through to look at the first five photos. First five photos would get them sucked in. Just think Instagram." — **2025-12-09**

---

## 2. Holding Costs Will Kill You

This lesson appears in virtually every spec session. The message is consistent: time on market erodes profit faster than most builders anticipate, and the willingness to take a loss and redeploy capital beats holding out for a target price.

> "The people that just sold it took the loss, they turned and turned and they turned their money like 10 times. So you're better off taking the small loss and just move on." — **2025-12-09**

> "Holding costs will kill you." — **2026-01-13 — Spec Build Finishes, Budget Strategy, and Deal Financing**

The **2025-11-11** roundtable added the perspective that one participant floated a spec for 18 months and barely broke even, leading to the blunt conclusion: *"You can't count on the income from a spec home."*

---

## 3. Build for a Known Buyer, Not for Yourself

Multiple sessions stress that spec homes fail when builders design for their own taste rather than reverse-engineering for a specific buyer profile.

> "We are building for a buyer here and with the belief that it will suit the buyer and it will move. When people build not for the buyer, that's when you see it sit." — **2026-04-14 — Spec Builder Roundtable: Lot Selection, Market Conditions, and Warranty Management**

The **2026-01-13** session extended this to finish selection, where builders discussed the "touch-and-feel" principle — spending money where buyers physically interact with the home (hardware, fixtures, flooring) rather than on invisible upgrades nobody notices:

> "If someone thinks that you're skimping on something that's like between $20 and $100, then like what else are you skimping on?"

A related cautionary tale from **2025-12-09**: David's designer was based in L.A. and pushed cutting-edge design, but *"everybody in Boulder wanted stuff that was in style like five years ago,"* resulting in a home that sat unsold for six months.

---

## 4. Location and Lot Quality Are Non-Negotiable

Lot selection comes up in every roundtable as the highest-leverage decision a spec builder makes. The lesson is consistent: you cannot overcome a bad location with good construction.

> "There are some things that you can't fix. You can't make prettier. You can't make a buyer get over." — **2026-04-14**

The group developed a practical checklist approach — power lines, sex offenders, halfway houses, corner lots, school proximity, lot shape — because in the heat of deal flow, these factors are easy to overlook. The **2025-12-09** session codified the "A-Plus Everything Rule":

> "If you've got like two A's and a negative or a B or a C, then those are going to sit."

And from **2026-04-14**, one builder's confession about buying a triangle-shaped corner lot at a great price:

> "I was 40,000 in before I was 200,000 in before we even broke ground. So that was very expensive."

The land-cost rule of thumb from **2025-12-09** — pay no more than 20-25% of the projected ARV for dirt — was shared as a standard guardrail.

---

## 5. Pre-Selling Creates Enormous Complexity

Every session where pre-selling is discussed reaches the same conclusion: it dramatically increases the builder's workload relative to a pure spec build with minimal or no margin benefit.

> "It took 4 million percent more effort on my part to get it to the finish line than a spec house. Every decision has to run by the husband, the wife, the kid, the aunt, the uncle, their friend is an architect." — **2025-11-11**

The advice, repeated across **2025-11-11**, **2026-01-13**, and **2026-02-10**, is: if you do pre-sell, take a large non-refundable deposit (around 20%), limit customization to cosmetic finishes only, convert all selections to allowances, and have a clear change order process. Otherwise, *"you might as well just build a custom house."* — **2026-03-10**

---

## 6. Financing Structures and Investor Relationships

The **2025-11-11** roundtable established the core financing frameworks that were revisited in later sessions:

**The 50/50 Partnership Model:**
> "I use partners on all my new builds. So I come in with no money out of pocket. Partner gets 50% of the profit. I get 50% of profit. I do charge a GC fee of 10% ish of the build price and I do get a real estate commission on the backend."

**The 25% Investor Return Threshold:**
> "25 is the number. Below that it's tough to find money."

**Bank Relationships Over Single Lender Dependency:**
> "There's three banks that I'm using right now because periodically the banks stop lending on new construction for spec. So you have to build relationships with several banks." — **2025-11-11**

A critical insight from the same session: if you already own lots free and clear, using them as collateral for a bank loan is dramatically cheaper than giving up 50% equity to a private investor.

The **2026-01-13** session added tactical detail about structuring draws so builders can pay themselves a GC fee through the construction loan, with banks generally accepting PM and superintendent fees but not overhead profit before sale.

---

## 7. Profit Margin Targets

Across sessions, the benchmarks are consistent:

- **Target 30% profit margin, accept 20%:** "The number to shoot for is 30. You might end up with 20." — **2025-12-09**
- **20% return on total project costs** (including interest, selling fees, holding costs) as the developer minimum — **2025-11-11**

---

## 8. Spec Building Is Dramatically Simpler Than Custom — If You Keep It That Way

This is a recurring observation from experienced spec builders:

> "Building on spec is nine billion times easier than building anything for a customer. It's not even remotely close." — **2026-03-10**

The simplicity advantage disappears the moment buyers get involved mid-build. The **2026-03-10** session captured a horror story: *"She walked in and said I hate it — rip it out. She's currently on the second tile she's ripped out and destroyed. He's like, it's crushing my soul."*

Practical advice from the same session: complete all material selections and begin purchasing before breaking ground so you know you'll be on budget.

---

## 9. Staging Matters — But Context Dependent

The consensus across **2026-03-10** and **2025-12-09** is that staging is nearly essential in competitive markets with inventory:

> "Staging a house — night and day difference — because they can't envision anything."

But in low-inventory markets, the impact diminishes: *"There's so few houses that if someone wants a house — I haven't staged a house in years."* — **2026-03-10**

---

## 10. The Spec Home as an Accidental Business Development Tool

Even when a spec doesn't sell quickly, it functions as a model home and marketing asset. From **2026-01-13**:

> "It has acted as a model home and I've gotten a handful of builds in the last six months just from walking people through it."

And from **2025-11-11**:

> "It'll give you a lot of credibility in the construction world and the real estate world. My average price point on the sales side just skyrocketed. Start doing spec houses and it'll open up a lot of doors for you."

---

## 11. Budget-First Thinking, Not Design-First

The **2026-02-10** session crystallized what experienced spec builders do:

> "I know what I can spend to build it. The budget's 2.5. And then we have to fit all the design stuff into it. I'm budget first."

This is reinforced by the **2026-01-13** discussion about assigning line-item budgets to designers rather than global budgets, and using recognizable brand anchoring (Wolf, Sub-Zero) to signal quality while value-engineering elsewhere.

---

## Summary Pattern

The spec building sessions collectively teach a philosophy of **disciplined restraint**: sell finished not in-progress, pick A-grade lots, build for a specific buyer, keep it simple, protect capital velocity, and treat every hold day as money lost. The builders who struggle are almost always the ones who deviated from one of these principles — listing too early, over-designing for a market, holding too long, or letting a pre-sale buyer turn a spec into a custom project.

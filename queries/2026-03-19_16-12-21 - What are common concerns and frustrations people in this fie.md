# Common Concerns and Frustrations in Custom Home Building & Design

Based on a comprehensive review of all Builder's Edge coaching sessions, here are the recurring concerns and frustrations that participants bring up across the program. These represent the real, lived challenges of builders, designers, and construction professionals at various stages of business.

---

## 1. Client Budget Misalignment and Sticker Shock

**This is arguably the single most pervasive frustration across the entire program.** Clients consistently underestimate what their dream home will cost, and the builder is often the one who has to deliver the bad news.

> "Clients think that just because it's on a plan it's like done. But it's not. We have to open the book up for them." — Erin, *2025-05-29 — Budget vs. Actual*

Specific manifestations include:
- **Architects designing beyond budgets**: Designers and architects create concepts that far exceed what clients said they could spend, leaving the builder to reconcile the gap (*2025-04-15*, *2025-07-24*, *2024-03-20*).
- **Clients disengaging after receiving estimates**: Rather than working through the numbers collaboratively, clients go silent to "figure it out," leaving builders in scheduling and commitment limbo (*2025-06-19 — Open Workshop*).
- **Inexperienced architects inflating expectations**: David in Boulder noted a "consistent pattern where prospective clients believe a project will cost half of what it actually will, due to an influx of inexperienced architects post-Marshall fire and post-COVID who are designing homes without understanding current build costs" (*2025-07-24*).
- **The $2–5M stress zone**: Erin repeatedly identifies this price range as uniquely difficult because clients are spending a large percentage of their net worth, creating financial stress that manifests as excessive scrutiny (*2025-09-11*, *2025-10-02*).

> "That client is spending a large percentage of their net worth on this home. So that creates stress for that client, which then can look like nickel and diming to the builder." — Erin, *2025-09-11*

---

## 2. Subcontractor Reliability and Management

**Trades not showing up, not finishing on schedule, and not meeting quality standards** is a near-universal frustration that surfaces in almost every open workshop.

- **Unresponsive subs**: Builders invest time sending plans and following up, only to get ghosted. Erin's structured follow-up cadence (Day 3, 5, 10) was developed specifically to combat this (*2025-04-04*, *2025-10-02*).
- **Overpromising timelines**: Trades routinely give optimistic timelines they cannot meet. Erin teaches builders to pad schedules based on known patterns rather than accepting trade promises at face value (*2025-09-18*).

> "Hey, so-and-so, I know you told me three weeks. I'm going to say it's nine weeks. Does that sound reasonable? Like stop them in their tracks. Don't ever think they're telling you the truth." — Erin, *2025-09-18*

- **Quality drop-offs**: "They're awesome until they're not" is a recurring Erin phrase. Even well-vetted subs eventually underperform, and builders need to be ready (*2025-04-22*).
- **Subs overbilling**: Trades invoicing for more work than they've completed is a persistent financial risk (*2025-10-16*, *2025-10-23*).
- **Trade resistance to systems**: Subs resist adopting software, documentation processes, or even email. Phone calls and in-person communication remain the only reliable methods (*2025-04-04*, *2025-10-02*).

> "The language of construction is not email. We all know that. We can try to fight it all day long. But the deal is, it's a phone call in person, track you down." — Erin, *2025-10-02*

---

## 3. Difficult, Demanding, or Toxic Clients

**Managing the emotional and psychological dimensions of client relationships** is a constant source of stress, especially as project values increase.

- **Clients who become hostile**: Erica described a "high-profile but verbally abusive designer client in LA who called her team 'amateur hour'" (*2025-04-22*). Marla described a client who locked her out and refused to pay vendors over a stone issue she didn't cause (*2026-01-29*).
- **Clients who hold mistakes over the builder**: Even after errors are resolved and paid for, some clients use them as leverage indefinitely (*2025-03-31*).

> "That will drip like a little faucet and then it'll be in a, some things expected. Kind of holding something over you." — Erin, *2025-03-31*

- **Clients who refuse to share budgets**: Denise described a prospect who "absolutely refused to share her budget, making it impossible to scope the design appropriately" (*2025-08-07*).
- **Clients who bring in their own contractors or materials**: This creates warranty gaps, coordination nightmares, and financial exposure for the builder (*2025-07-17*, *2026-02-10*).

> "How many times can I just keep showing up for something that you ordered that I haven't charged you for?" — Participant, *2026-02-10*

- **Emotional fatigue on long projects**: Both builders and clients lose motivation toward the end of expensive, long-duration custom builds (*2025-05-08*).

## 4. Getting Paid for Pre-Construction Work

**Builders consistently express frustration about investing significant time in pricing, estimating, and pre-construction coordination — often for free — with no guarantee of being hired.**

- Brooke described "doing significant pre-construction design and selection work — including selecting hard and soft finishes — without being compensated" (*2025-05-15*).
- David noted that pre-construction phases can stretch over a year with minimal financial protection if the client walks away (*2025-11-13*).
- Cammie discovered her pre-construction phases were "taking up to a year with only a $25K credited deposit to show for it" (*2025-09-11*).
- Brett experienced clients using pre-construction work to solicit bids from other builders at a lower price (*2025-07-24*).

> "I'm not looking to get $500 so I can go price a set of schematic drawings. I want you to really go talk to three other builders — and if we're going to go into pre-construction, it's because you want to work with me." — Erin, *2025-07-24*

## 5. Architects Delivering Incomplete or Inadequate Plans

**This is a structural frustration in the industry** — architects frequently deliver plan sets that are insufficient for accurate pricing or construction.

- "Architects often provide incomplete pricing sets (60–70% of information needed), making accurate budgeting difficult" (*2025-04-04*).
- A participant described an architect who "refuses to provide adequate drawings for pricing, saying he won't do more work until he knows if the project is in budget — creating a circular standoff" (*2025-05-15*).
- Ken Newberry (guest architect) acknowledged this is common: "Someone is going to have to make these decisions. Do you want the plumber to make the decision? Do you want the cabinet guy to make the decision?" (*2026-02-05*).
- Hand-drawn architectural sets are frequently internally inconsistent, requiring daily field interpretation (*2026-03-05*).

---

## 6. Scaling Challenges: Delegation, Hiring, and the Owner Bottleneck

**The tension between maintaining quality and letting go** is a defining struggle for builders trying to grow.

- **Christine described being overwhelmed** trying to personally manage client meetings, trade questions, staff direction, and email volume across 6-7 projects at $5M+ (*2025-03-31*). Erin's blunt assessment: "You just have a people problem. You need people."
- **Fear of delegation**: Builders struggle to accept that a PM getting 80% of the way there is a win, not a failure (*2025-07-31*).

> "If anyone here wants to scale you have to learn to step away period or you'll never grow." — Erin, *2025-07-31*

- **Finding qualified project managers**: This frustration appears in nearly every workshop. Denise lost "a well-compensated PM of five years who ultimately didn't want the responsibility level" (*2025-04-09*). Multiple participants describe the difficulty of finding PMs who combine field expertise, communication skills, and an ownership mindset (*2025-11-06*, *2025-11-25*).
- **The owner as bottleneck**: Sandra noted she "pauses all projects when traveling because she is a one-person operation" (*2025-07-31*). Erin herself says: "There's no vacation land. It's how can I be more efficient knowing what I know now" (*2025-05-08*).

## 7. Fee Justification and Competitive Pressure

**Builders struggle to explain and defend their management fees**, especially against competitors who charge less or structure fees differently.

- Ryan found that "project management fees are not yet standard in his market" at the $2-2.5M level (*2026-02-12*).
- Ashley operates in a small market where "clients don't understand or value construction management fees" (*2025-09-11*).
- Participants repeatedly raised the discomfort of clients questioning whether the management fee is already covered by the builder's profit (*2025-05-01*).

> "People would never ask their attorney or their physician, what's your margin on my job? How many other industries out there would even be willing to share what your profit is?" — Erin, *2025-10-16*

The solution Erin teaches is to **sell through systems, not price** — presenting the full suite of deliverables (weekly reports, four-week calendars, budget tracking) so clients see what the fee buys before the number is discussed (*2025-10-16*, *2025-10-30*).

## 8. Cash Flow Volatility and Financial Uncertainty

**Construction cash flow is inherently lumpy**, and this creates persistent anxiety.

- Erica and Beth "experienced a period with no work in December and are concerned about business consistency" (*2025-04-09*).
- Ryan (Providence Homes) was "operating on very thin margins and is unsure what a healthy operating cost percentage looks like" (*2025-09-04*).
- High-net-worth clients delay decisions, "resulting in poor cash flow in early months of the year" (*2025-09-22*).
- Spec builders face the risk of holding costs destroying profitability: "Holding costs will kill you" (*2025-12-09*).

> "Plenty of times I've looked at cash flow and I'm like, am I going to make it? Truly, am I going to make it?" — Erin, *2025-09-18*

---

## 9. Designer and Architect Coordination Failures

**The builder often gets stuck in the middle** between designers who work at their own pace and clients who expect the builder to keep things moving.

- Designers consistently deliver selections late, "causing the builder's project manager to drop core responsibilities like scheduling and quality control in order to chase down information" (*2025-12-04*).
- Erin frames this as a fundamental difference in wiring: "Builders are built to move projects forward crazy efficiently. Designers are built because they have extraordinary vision and creativity and things have to bubble... two different ball games" (*2025-12-04*).
- Designers create concepts that dramatically exceed budgets "with no material specs provided" (*2025-04-15*).
- In smaller markets like Huntsville, Alabama, "clients are not accustomed to hiring designers early in the process and resist the additional time and cost" (*2025-06-19*).

---

## 10. Navigating External Disruptions (Tariffs, Market Shifts, Material Surprises)

**Forces outside the builder's control** create significant stress and difficult client conversations.

- **Tariffs**: A builder in Canada faced 25% tariffs with "vendors pushing cost increases onto them and clients resisting absorbing those costs." Erin taught separating fear charges from real costs: "It would be like me turning a contract into a client and putting a line item for fear and then plugging in a hundred thousand dollars. That's what vendors are doing to builders" (*2025-04-15*).
- **Natural material variation**: Clients picking natural stone or wood "frequently expect perfect, uniform appearance and are unhappy when natural variation shows up on site" (*2025-04-04*).
- **Slow or shifting markets**: Multiple participants in Austin, Boulder, and other markets describe pipelines drying up, spec homes sitting unsold, and the difficulty of rebuilding feeder relationships after market disruptions (*2026-02-26*, *2025-12-09*).

---

## 11. Emotional and Personal Toll

**This business is deeply personal and physically demanding**, and participants regularly surface the human cost.

- Renee described "a prolonged difficult season with challenges across multiple areas — closing jobs, staying on budget, a client dispute, and even a nine-month battle with Google Business verification" (*2025-12-18*).
- Julie described quitting her tech career and dealing with a stress-related medical condition from running on "high gear for so many years" (*2026-01-29*).
- The feeling that "entrepreneurial success looks easy for everyone else on social media while personally struggling with the grind" was raised directly (*2025-07-10*).

> "I've tried to talk myself into this being an easy business. I am totally honest with myself now — this is a really difficult business for a lot of reasons." — Erin, *2025-04-04*

---

## 12. Social Media and Visibility Anxiety

**Builders know they should be posting but struggle with it** — fear of being on camera, perfectionism, time constraints, and uncertainty about what to share.

- "Impostor syndrome and uncertainty about whether personal content adds value" (*2025-06-10*).
- "Fear of being on camera and perfectionism causing paralysis — taking dozens of takes or abandoning content entirely before posting" (*2025-06-10*).
- Denise shared that she "has never marketed her 20-year business and does not know how to tell her own story" (*2025-08-14*).

> "The only one getting in the way of it is you. It just — I'm the bottleneck. I know that you are." — Erin, *2025-06-10*

## Summary: The Underlying Pattern

Across all of these frustrations, Erin consistently identifies a common thread: **most problems stem from a lack of systems, a lack of clarity in communication, or a failure to set expectations upfront.** The Builder's Edge program is fundamentally designed to address these root causes — through structured processes, transparent client communication, and the personal discipline to implement them consistently.

> "95% of the industry doesn't do any of this. So, literally, just by checking the boxes here, you're now in the top 5%, maybe top 3% of how you can serve clients." — Erin, *2025-09-18*

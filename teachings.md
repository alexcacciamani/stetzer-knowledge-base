## Pricing & Proposals

### Brand Identity and Personal-Business Alignment
*Session: 2024-03-20 — Builder's Edge Program Kickoff Call*

In today's world, a builder's personal identity and company brand are inseparable. Authenticity comes from aligning who you are with how your business presents itself, rather than treating them as separate entities. Clients and partners connect with the real person behind the company.

> Aligning your identity with your brand, because they're one in the same, it's not company and it's not person. It's one thing. And so all of that needs to align for you to really grow your business, because that is authentic.

### Making the Jump to High-End Clientele
*Session: 2024-03-20 — Builder's Edge Program Kickoff Call*

Transitioning to top-tier clients is a deliberate leap that requires doing advance relationship work with high-caliber architects and designers, studying their standards, and continuing to take necessary work in the meantime. The jump does not happen passively — it requires sustained effort before the opportunity arrives.

> I identified five architects that I'm going to work with. And I identified five designers and I studied their work. I marched into their offices and said, I want to work with you. I don't know if I'm here yet, but if there's anything, any project you want me to bid on, I'm in.

### Distinguishing Edge Cases from Chronic Problems
*Session: 2024-03-20 — Builder's Edge Program Kickoff Call*

Not every difficult client situation warrants walking away. The key distinction is between clients who are demanding but have legitimate needs versus clients who are genuinely hostile or unethical. Edge cases should be accommodated with adjusted systems; truly toxic clients should be severed regardless of financial cost.

> There's a big difference between wanting factual information and being really mean. And when we get into like the mean evil zone, no way gone. So having said that, now we know good project from bad project.

### Documenting Construction Administration Interactions
*Session: 2024-03-20 — Builder's Edge Program Kickoff Call*

When clients receive unexpectedly large construction administration bills from architects, it can damage trust in the builder. Proactively documenting every architect or designer engagement with a clear reason — such as a plan discrepancy or missing detail — protects the builder, creates transparency, and prevents misunderstandings about who is responsible for costs.

> Every time you engage with an architect or a designer, it's documented and the note should say something like the detail was not clear, or I didn't have a detail to execute, or the dimensions on the plans don't match the dimensions on the elevations.

### Closing the Loop on Mistakes Without Reopening the Wound
*Session: 2025-03-31 — Open Workshop: Mistake Recovery, Cost Plus Pricing, and Managing Client Time*

When a builder makes a mistake, addresses it honestly, presents solutions, and absorbs the cost, the matter should be considered closed. Offering additional compensation or continuing to apologize after resolution risks highlighting the error further and undermining confidence in the relationship.

> I would never drag anything else into it because that like, to me, when we start, like, do you just feel bad or you're worried about the relationship?

### Fixed Price vs. Cost Plus Based on Certainty Level
*Session: 2025-03-31 — Open Workshop: Mistake Recovery, Cost Plus Pricing, and Managing Client Time*

Fixed price contracts work best when project details are highly certain, allowing the builder to protect margins. Cost plus is better suited for projects with significant unknowns, such as large acreage sites, because it protects the builder from absorbing unpredictable site costs while being transparent with the client.

> Fixed price is great when you're pretty close to a hundred percent certain. And it seems like how that model works well for you and your profit margins.

### Transparency of the Insurance Premium in Fixed Price
*Session: 2025-03-31 — Open Workshop: Mistake Recovery, Cost Plus Pricing, and Managing Client Time*

In a fixed price model, the builder must include a risk buffer to account for market changes and unknowns. Erin teaches clients that this buffer is essentially an insurance policy for both parties, and making that explicit removes misunderstanding about why fixed price may cost more upfront.

> It makes sense for everybody because in a fixed price model, there's an insurance policy in there. I've even explained that if you want to go fixed price, then there's a huge insurance policy here for all of us. And that's in the cost.

### Cost Plus Fee Structure Including Project Manager Cost
*Session: 2025-03-31 — Open Workshop: Mistake Recovery, Cost Plus Pricing, and Managing Client Time*

In a cost plus arrangement, the builder's fee sits above the cost of work and must cover the project manager's fully loaded cost, general liability, and profit. The project manager cost can either be embedded in the cost of work or listed separately as its own line item so clients understand what they are paying for.

> There's a fee. So cost plus, then the fee is charged to that cost below that is our project manager cost, our general liability cost and our profit.

### Pre-Construction Strategic Plan to Flush Out Unknowns
*Session: 2025-03-31 — Open Workshop: Mistake Recovery, Cost Plus Pricing, and Managing Client Time*

Before committing to a full service scope on a complex project, charging a fixed conceptual or pre-construction fee to document all unknowns, align all parties, and set expectations reduces risk for the builder, architect, designer, and client. This phase produces documentation that guides the entire team before detailed design begins.

> It happens before we get into the detailed design pieces of it, but it helps flesh out and really level expectations across all of the parties, the client, the contractor, the architect, the interior designer.

### Good Better Best Finish Budgeting for Early Scope Control
*Session: 2025-03-31 — Open Workshop: Mistake Recovery, Cost Plus Pricing, and Managing Client Time*

Assigning a good, better, or best finish category to each specification area early in the design process allows the team to set realistic budget ranges before final selections are made. This prevents designers from over-specifying in areas where budget has already been committed to structural or engineering needs.

> We look at it as is it going to be like a good, better or best kind of finish option, which allows us to put kind of a budget number and risk assessment with those items.

### Scaling Out of the Bottleneck with Project Managers and an Executive Assistant
*Session: 2025-03-31 — Open Workshop: Mistake Recovery, Cost Plus Pricing, and Managing Client Time*

When a builder is the sole point of contact for clients, trades, and staff across multiple large projects, every constraint runs through one person. Hiring experienced project managers and an executive assistant who filters and categorizes communications are the two primary levers that free the owner to operate at a higher level.

> You just have a people problem. You need people.

### Pricing as a Structured Team Process
*Session: 2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough*

Collecting bids and pricing a new project should follow a documented, repeatable system that tracks each trade and vendor through defined stages: plans sent, receipt confirmed, quote received, quote reviewed, quote approved, and budget updated. This ensures no bottlenecks are missed and every team member knows the status at a glance.

> The purpose of this document is one, to have a system when you get bigger and have multiple people working on something.

### Assigning the Right Trade to the Right Project
*Session: 2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough*

Not every subcontractor is a fit for every project. Builders should deliberately match trades and vendors to each project's scale, style, and timeline. Using the wrong contractor—even a skilled one—can cause delays and misalignment.

> Even if it's a bathroom remodel, we may have a trim carpenter that is better suited for small remodels. So to plug in a trim carpenter that has a whole workshop and is working on massive projects—that may cause a delay in the project.

### Proactive Follow-Up Cadence with Trades
*Session: 2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough*

Waiting passively for bids doesn't work. A structured follow-up on day 3 (confirm receipt by phone), day 5 (check for questions/progress), and day 10 (escalate if nothing received) keeps the process moving and signals to trades that there is a real deadline. Phone calls are more effective than emails because email is easy to ignore.

> Day three, it's really important to make that phone call saying, hey stucco person, did you actually receive the plans? Email gets bogged down. It's easy to say you didn't receive an email.

### Budget Only Populated with Approved Quotes
*Session: 2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough*

The working budget should only be updated with finalized, approved quotes—not preliminary or partial ones. This prevents inaccurate numbers from entering the budget and creating downstream problems with clients.

> We don't populate the budget with all these other actions of no's, right? We only populate the budget with yeses.

### Three to Four Week Pricing Timeline as a Goal
*Session: 2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough*

With a well-prepared subcontractor pricing guide and organized project documents sent upfront, a full pricing cycle from plans sent to approved quotes should take three to four weeks. This requires disciplined follow-up and project preparation in advance.

> The typical turnaround we want to stay within is three to four weeks. And it's very doable and very possible when the project is set up really well with the subcontractor pricing guide.

### Subs Own Their Estimating Liability
*Session: 2025-04-04 — Pricing A New Project Playbook Walkthrough*

Builders should not absorb the risk of subcontractor estimating errors. Providing detailed plans, specs, and follow-up questions places accountability on the sub to produce an accurate quote. If a sub misses something, the builder's documented outreach and follow-up process protects them from absorbing the cost.

> We actually don't want the liability of doing any estimating. So we put all of the weight on the people doing the work.

Whether to charge for the pricing and estimating process is not a fixed rule—it depends on the builder's current goals, the relationship with the architect or client, and the stage of the business. A builder seeking volume or new relationships may bid for free; one with a full pipeline may charge a fee.

> To me, that answer is based on your yearly goals, which we should be looking at. I look at mine every day.

Excel and phone calls outperform project management software for most builders because trades are not tech-friendly, automated emails create notification fatigue, and platform data often becomes inaccurate when not maintained in real time. > We're 100% Excel and our, and it's a very simple—we really simplify each of our processes because they work. And so if something, as you all know, if something works in this industry, you stay with it.

### Cross-Trade Impact Awareness
*Session: 2025-04-17 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide Walkthrough*

A key discipline in pricing is understanding how one trade's decisions affect other trades. For example, decorative registers affect drywall pricing, trimless lights require level-five finish sheet rockers, and baseboard height affects where electricians place outlets. Capturing these details in a shared document prevents missed scope and budget overruns.

> The gap in pricing is when we don't provide this information and the painter doesn't know that the doors are stained and then there's a nice little surprise later on.

### Builder Controls the Pricing Document
*Session: 2025-04-17 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide Walkthrough*

Subcontractors are never given direct input access to the pricing guide. The builder controls all data entry to maintain accuracy and prevent subs from adjusting their prices based on what budget figures they see. Transparency of pricing to clients and the team is handled separately through the budget model.

> We don't let the subs input anything we control the entire document so that we're in control of the interpretation.

### The Modification System for Construction-Phase Changes
*Session: 2025-04-17 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide Walkthrough*

Once construction begins, the subcontractor pricing guide is set aside. All changes during construction are managed through a separate modification system: project managers log action items, collect pricing for changes, and escalate to a cross-functional team review before a change order is submitted to the homeowner for approval. Nothing moves forward without documented approval.

> We take modifications really seriously and I would think we probably have hundreds of modifications if I were to actually go back and take a look at some of the projects.

### Role Separation Between Field PMs and Office Pricing Team
*Session: 2025-04-17 — Subcontractor Pricing Guide Walkthrough*

Project managers are kept out of the subcontractor pricing guide entirely — they are not brought into a project until construction is about to begin. The pricing document is handled exclusively by the in-office team. This keeps field PMs focused on problem-solving and documentation on site, and pricing specialists focused on budget accuracy.

> The subcontractor pricing guide we don't even review that with the project manager. We don't even bring the project manager into the project until we're ready.

### Three Rules of Budget Presentation
*Session: 2025-05-01 — Creating a Conceptual Budget*

Before presenting any budget to a client, Erin emphasizes three guiding principles: always be of service (not focused on winning the job), always seek clarity (even when you think you have it), and always leave people in a better place than before they met you. These rules reframe the budget meeting as a service act rather than a sales pitch.

> If you're there for service and you're there for clarity by the time you leave that meeting you will have done your job.

### Price Certainty Column
*Session: 2025-05-01 — Creating a Conceptual Budget*

The most important column in Erin's budget is a percentage representing how certain she is about each line item. This column forces transparent communication about the quality of each number — whether it's a hard bid, historical data, or an allowance — and was the key differentiator that helped her win work with high-end clients who wanted to understand how numbers were derived.

> I just got really really mad one day and I'm like if nobody else is going to talk about this then I'm going to talk about it because no other builders are talking about this so I'm just going to explain how I got the numbers.

### Zero Percent Certainty as a Signal of Exclusion
*Session: 2025-05-01 — Creating a Conceptual Budget*

When a line item is assigned zero percent certainty, it signals to the client that this scope is not included in the current quote rather than that the builder is unsure. This practice ensures clients understand what is missing from the budget, which Erin argues is as important as communicating what is included.

> People appreciate a lot of what's in the quote but they appreciate even more what's not and we owe them that conversation.

### Verification Spreadsheets for Complex Scopes
*Session: 2025-05-01 — Creating a Conceptual Budget*

For high-risk or detail-heavy line items such as windows and roofing, Erin creates a separate verification spreadsheet that is distributed to the architect, designer, client, and builder. This ensures all parties confirm specifications before money is committed, protecting the builder from liability when discrepancies exist on plans.

> I will tell you 100 percent of the time what's on the plans changes and so it's a huge step to take because you don't want to order these windows and have a surprise later.

### Contingency as a Communication Tool
*Session: 2025-05-01 — Creating a Conceptual Budget*

The contingency and miscellaneous line ideally sits around one percent on a well-defined project, but should be raised to eight percent or higher on early-stage or information-poor feasibility budgets. The contingency percentage is a direct signal to clients about how much is unknown, and adjusting it is a way to protect clients who may be seeking bank financing.

> That contingency number which really wants to land around one percent ideally... we probably need to plug in eight percent like that's where I would communicate the guard rails for this project.

### Micro-Teams to Prevent Trade Gaps

> Painter you're amazing, who's your favorite trim carpenter? I want to go meet the trim carpenter you love. That person's on the job you can't wait to get started because you can do your job efficiently.

### The Diamond of Why – Four Categories of Modifications
*Session: 2025-05-15 — Modifications Control the Process – Tracking Changes and Budget Management*

Erin uses a framework called the 'Diamond of Why' to categorize why modifications happen: (1) something was added or deleted, (2) a design element won't work in the field as drawn, (3) a detail wasn't fully developed yet at the time of budgeting, and (4) external/physical forces like weather, market conditions, or unforeseen site conditions. Documenting the 'why' behind every modification helps owners, project managers, and clients understand and accept changes.

> The biggest thing is talking about why things happen. So any owner, project manager, money manager, everybody sees it that we start one path and now we're on a different path.

### Contingency as a Buffer for Estimating Errors and Minor Incidents

> Our contingency is for estimating errors because we're going to be some stuff we leave out or we miss. And we'd rather pull that out of contingency than make that into a whole modification.

### Sticker Stacking for Proper Acclimation
*Session: 2025-12-11 — Industry Pro Expert: Doro Rojas on Wood Flooring, Finishes, and Installation*

When storing wood on-site prior to installation, boards should be sticker stacked, meaning alternating layers are placed perpendicular to each other to allow airflow through the entire stack. > You put one like this and then the other one the opposite way because you need that airflow to go right through the whole entire flooring.

All wood floors will amber or yellow over time to some degree. The best way to slow this is to neutralize the tannic acids in the wood and use a water-based finish product rather than oil-based. > How do you prevent a floor from amber or to turn yellow is how you neutralize everything that you do... the best product for that is to stick to a water base and to neutralize all the acids on the wood the tannic acids.
